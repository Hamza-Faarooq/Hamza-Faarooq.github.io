The Mathematical Pivot: Conquering the 50% Sparsity Wall in Spiking Neural Networks

From catastrophic solver failures to state-of-the-art iterative pruning on real-world neuromorphic datasets.
Research Internship under Shuai Lee & Ameer Hamza Khan | University of Oulu [cite: 102]
By: MUHAMMAD HAMZA
INDIAN INSTITUTE OF TECHNOLOGY, KHARAGPUR

Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) offer a tantalizing promise for the future of AI: brain-like, asynchronous computation that draws orders of magnitude less power than standard artificial neural networks (ANNs). However, deploying SNNs onto severely energy-constrained neuromorphic hardware requires rigorous network sparsity. In this research log, I document the journey of developing a novel Criticality-Constrained Quadratic Pruning (CQP) method, the brutal mathematical realities of convex optimization bugs, and how we finally conquered the 50% sparsity wall.

🔬 Phase 1 & 2: The Baseline Architectures
Due to initial environment restrictions, we began our sanity checks using a complex synthetic dataset (`sklearn.make_classification`) with 784 features[cite: 4, 5, 122]. We established a 3-layer fully-connected baseline (784 → 256 → 128 → 10)[cite: 11]. Utilizing a direct current injection encoding scheme, our baseline SNN achieved an impressive 92.25% test accuracy, outperforming its ANN (ReLU) counterpart (90.45%)[cite: 12, 14].

1. Quantifying Importance: Criticality and Time

In standard Deep Learning, we compress networks using simple Magnitude (L1) Pruning—discarding weights closest to zero. But in an SNN, is raw weight magnitude the true indicator of a synapse's importance?

To answer this, we calculated surrogate gradients of the loss with respect to incoming weights to determine neuron "criticality"[cite: 18]. When evaluating our baseline, we found that the minimum criticality score for an input neuron was a staggering 0.649[cite: 23]. This empirically proved that the SNN was highly distributed, utilizing almost all input dimensions effectively[cite: 24, 26].

Simultaneously, we tackled temporal redundancy. By calculating the KL-divergence between output spike distributions of consecutive timesteps, we identified that the network reached 95% of its cumulative divergence by step 9 (out of 10)[cite: 30, 32]. Truncating this redundant step immediately yielded a free 10% theoretical energy saving on neuromorphic hardware without altering a single weight[cite: 32, 131].

2. The Crisis: The CVXPY Solver and the 50% Wall

To prune the network while protecting these highly critical neurons, we initially formulated the problem as a Convex Quadratic Program solved via CVXPY. The objective was to minimize the squared magnitude of pruned weights, subject to a hard constraint: if a neuron's score crossed a threshold ($\tau = 0.9$), its mask must remain $m_i \ge 0.5$[cite: 61].

⚠️ The 50% Sparsity Collapse:
At 20% sparsity, CQP outperformed Magnitude pruning by +0.5%[cite: 40]. But at 50% target sparsity, disaster struck. Magnitude pruning held strong at 91.7%, but our CQP method violently collapsed to 48.0% accuracy[cite: 41, 142, 143].

Autopsy of a Mathematical Failure

We attempted to fix this by replacing the hard constraints with a soft penalty term ($\lambda \sum c_i (1 - m_i)$)[cite: 184]. We ran a $\lambda$ sweep at a fixed 50% sparsity to find the perfect balance[cite: 240]. However, looking at the results where $\lambda = 0.0$ (which mathematically should have equaled exactly 50% sparsity and 91.7% accuracy), the solver outputted 62.0% sparsity and 47.1% accuracy[cite: 251, 252, 253].

This exposed a fatal flaw in our methodology: The Continuous Relaxation Trap. Network pruning is a Mixed-Integer Programming (MIP) problem, which is NP-hard[cite: 256]. The solver bypassed this by allowing fractional masks (e.g., $m_i = 0.49$)[cite: 258, 259]. When we brutally rounded these fractions to binary floats in Python, we destroyed the optimal solution and accidentally deleted massive portions of the network[cite: 260, 261].

3. The Pivot: Combined Scoring and Iterative Healing

We completely abandoned the external CVXPY solver[cite: 263]. Instead, we implemented a mathematically sound Combined Importance Scoring metric directly in PyTorch[cite: 264]. We multiplied a neuron's weight magnitude by its criticality score, regulated by a hyperparameter $\alpha$:

$$ \text{Importance}_i = (W\_norm_i) \times (C\_score_i)^\alpha $$

By exactly sorting these scores, we mathematically guaranteed hitting our exact sparsity targets with zero rounding errors[cite: 267, 268]. However, a one-shot 50% cut using this new metric still only yielded 57.5% accuracy[cite: 320]. The culprit? Gradient Staleness. The criticality scores calculated at 0% sparsity become completely invalid the moment you delete 10% of the network, as the flow of information radically shifts[cite: 322, 323].

Crushing the "Zombie Weights" Bug

To solve this, we wrapped the metric in an Iterative Pruning Loop—slicing small chunks, fine-tuning the network to heal, recalculating fresh gradients, and repeating[cite: 279, 348]. During implementation, we encountered the "zombie weights" bug: the Adam optimizer's momentum was resurrecting weights we had set to zero[cite: 398, 399]. We fixed this by actively multiplying gradients by our pruning mask before `optimizer.step()` to ensure dead weights stayed dead[cite: 400, 401]. 

def iterative_cqp_prune(baseline_model, target_sparsity, trainloader, steps=3):
    # Gradually prunes the model to the target sparsity, fine-tuning in between [cite: 382]
    current_model = copy.deepcopy(baseline_model)
    sparsities = np.linspace(0, target_sparsity, steps + 1)[1:] # [cite: 383]
    
    for s in sparsities:
        fresh_scores = get_fresh_criticality_scores(current_model, trainloader) # [cite: 383]
        current_w_norms = current_model.fc1.weight.data.abs().mean(dim=0).cpu().numpy()
        
        current_model, actual_s, _ = cqp_prune(
            current_model, current_w_norms, fresh_scores, sparsity=s, alpha=1.0 # [cite: 383]
        )
        
        # Fine-tune to heal, masking gradients to prevent zombie weights [cite: 384, 401]
        fine_tune_with_masked_gradients(current_model, trainloader)
        
    return current_model, actual_s

4. Victory and the Transition to Real Data

The results of the new mathematical engine were staggering. At 60% sparsity, CQP achieved 91.6%, officially outperforming the Magnitude baseline (89.9%) by +1.7%[cite: 413, 414]. At 70% sparsity, while the Gradient-only baseline collapsed to random guessing (11.1%), CQP maintained an incredible 87.2% (+76.2% advantage)[cite: 415, 416].

With the algorithm mathematically sound, the final step was proving generalizability. We abandoned the synthetic data and rebuilt our pipeline to ingest the real MNIST and Fashion-MNIST datasets[cite: 440, 481, 482].

Because MNIST digits are centered with black borders, the network dynamically adapted—the surrogate gradients correctly assigned 0.0 criticality to the border pixels, allowing the pruning mask to instantly safely delete those weights[cite: 463, 464, 465]. As predicted, baseline accuracies shot up to the 96%-98% range [cite: 459], and our iterative CQP method seamlessly transferred its dominance over Magnitude pruning to the real-world benchmarks[cite: 448, 473].

From failing solvers to state-of-the-art sparse networks, the math now holds strong. We are ready to write the paper.
